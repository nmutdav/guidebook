Class Update - September 11, 2024

Wednesday, September 11, 2024

    Adjustment: To better align our projects with sound and media design specifically for theatre, our upcoming assignments will focus on using scripts as inspiration for creating soundscapes and animations. I will provide a repository of public domain scripts along with guidance on which excerpts to use. In class, we will discuss strategies for interpreting these excerpts and how to effectively render sound and animations that complement theatrical productions. This adjustment is intended to help you develop foundational skills in interpreting a script, conceiving of a design, and executing that design, preparing you for future opportunities in designing for theatrical productions. While the assignments will remain similar to before, they will now feature more structure, guidance, and context, framed through the lens of theatre design.

  • Assigned Short Play Scripts by Chekhov:
    • Group 1: "The Proposal"
    • Group 2: "The Wedding"
    • Group 3: "The Bear"
  • Students participated in a 45-minute breakout session to read, summarize, and brainstorm design concepts for their assigned scripts.
  • Upon returning to class, students created the following table to outline key elements of their scripts:
The Proposal The Wedding The Bear
Setting Farm, homestead, hunting grounds, meadows, wealth Room, large table, orchestra, wealth Parlor, wealth
Atmosphere Drama, intensity, comical, arguments Chaotic, superficial order contrasted with underlying chaos Funny, comical
Moments Five scenes with different arguments, marked by entrances and exits of characters Background orchestral music, intense moment at the end, uproar Broken chair, swell to emphasize intense moments

Based on these broad ideas, we discussed rendering animations and sounds to indicate or emphasize various situations. For instance, using projections of minimalist backdrops with colors or subtle animations to depict locations and create atmospheres, such as a meadow or a fancy parlor. Sound effects and animations can highlight key moments, such as the sound of a breaking chair, background orchestral music, or walla to give the impression of a dining room with people talking.

We also covered some sound effect terms including:

  • Walla: Ambient crowd noise, often used to simulate a bustling environment.
  • Risers: Gradually increasing sounds used to build tension or anticipation.

Additional resources mentioned:

  • IMSLP: A source for classical sheet music and recordings.
  • FreeSound.org: For finding sound effects.

Templates are now included in the curriculum to help kickstart your projects, including an essential basic sound effect player.

For your assignments, it's acceptable to create 3-5 very simplistic Processing IDE sketches that emphasize moments, settings, or atmospheres in your play. The interactivity within these sketches can be as simple as moving the mouse cursor to change animations or colors, and pressing a key to trigger sound effects.
